Abstract
This paper presents the Affective Coherence Monitoring (ACM), a biologically
inspired emotional intelligence engine designed for integration into transformer-
based AI architectures. ACM provides a mathematical framework for the real-time
analysis, regulation, and monitoring of emotional coherence in generated responses.
The mechanism operates through a system of coupled differential equations and
discrete modulation functions. Core emotional states are computed via a weighted
fusion of keyword-based lexica and dense semantic embeddings derived from a
384-dimensional sentence transformer ( all-MiniLM-L6-v2 ), where the semantic
similarity score is defined as ssem,e = (v text·ce+ 1.0)/2.0 using normalized emotion
centroidsc e. To model biological homeostasis, emotional states evolve according
to exponential decay dynamicsE t+∆t =E t·e−λ·∆t, whereλis an emotion-specific
decay tensor derived from a 4.0-second half-life.
Cross-emotion modulation functions govern the interaction between primary affects,
such as curiosity and joy, using piecewise additive rules with empirically defined
thresholds (e.g., s′

emotional incoherence through a context mismatch detector, which computes valence
conflict V C =min(Spos, Sneg)/(Stotal/2) and arousal disparity AD =|Ahigh−
Alow|/max (Ahigh+Alow,0.1) to classify alert levels into Nominal, Watch, or Alert
states. A habituation mechanism attenuates responses to repetitive stimuli via
a frequency-based piecewise function s′

Finally, the ACM modulates the generative temperature Tgendirectly based on the
alert level (e.g., Tgen= 0.3 for Alert states, Tgen= 0.7 for Nominal states). The
system is designed to identify emotional incongruities such as frustrated sarcasm
(MS <− 0.15) and emotional suppression ( AD > 0.6), enabling safer and more
coherent language generation. This framework bridges computational affect theory
with practical large language model safety mechanisms.

1. Introduction
Large language models (LLMs) have achieved remarkable capabilities in natural language
understanding and generation. Yet a persistent and underexplored challenge remains: the
emotional coherenceof generated responses. LLMs can produce text that is semantically
fluent while being affectively inconsistent—expressing enthusiasm when discussing tragedy,
or exhibiting detached neutrality in emotionally charged interactions. Such incoherence
undermines user trust, degrades the quality of human-AI interaction, and poses subtle
but significant risks in safety-critical deployments.
Existing approaches to affective computing in AI have largely focused onemotion recogni-
tion—classifying the emotional content of user inputs—rather than on the regulation and
coherence monitoring ofmodel outputs. This distinction is crucial. A model that correctly
identifies a user’s distress but responds with emotionally incongruent language fails at the
level of affective alignment, even if its informational content is accurate.
This paper introduces theAffective Coherence Monitoring (ACM), a novel com-
putational framework designed to address this gap. ACM is not a replacement for the
underlying language model; rather, it functions as a biologically inspired regulatory layer
that operates over the model’s generative process in real time. Drawing on principles
from affective neuroscience—specifically emotional homeostasis, habituation, and valence-
arousal dimensional theory [2]—ACM provides a rigorous mathematical apparatus for
monitoring, detecting, and correcting emotional incoherence in transformer-based AI
outputs.
The key contributions of this work are as follows:
1.Ahybrid emotion scoring systemthat fuses keyword-based lexical analysis with
dense semantic embeddings, enabling robust emotion detection across both surface-level
and latent textual features.
2.Abiologically motivated emotional dynamics modelincorporating exponential
decay (homeostasis) and frequency-based habituation, grounding the computational
framework in established models of affective regulation.
3.Acontext mismatch detectorthat quantifies valence conflict and arousal disparity
to classify emotional incoherence into actionable alert levels.
4.Atemperature modulation mechanismthat directly adjusts the language model’s
generation temperature based on detected emotional state, providing a closed-loop
feedback control system for affective coherence.
The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. Section 2 reviews related work in
affective computing and emotion-aware NLP. Section 3 presents the full mathematical
specification of the ACM system. Section 4 describes the system architecture and integra-
tion with transformer-based models. Section 5 interprets the implications for AI safety,
and Section 6 concludes with directions for future work.

2. Related Work
2.1 Affective Computing
The field of affective computing, pioneered by Picard [1], established the theoretical
and engineering foundations for systems that recognize, interpret, and simulate human
emotions. Early work focused on physiological signal processing and facial expression
recognition. More recent contributions have extended affective computing principles to
text-based interaction, forming the basis for sentiment analysis and emotion recognition
in NLP.
The dimensional model of emotion—most notably the valence-arousal circumplex proposed
by Russell [2]—provides a continuous, two-dimensional representation of emotional states.
Valence captures the positive-negative axis, while arousal represents the activation level
from calm to excited. ACM adopts this dimensional framework as its core representational
substrate, computing both valenceVand arousalAfrom discrete emotion scores.
2.2 Emotion Recognition in NLP
Emotion recognition in text has advanced substantially with the development of large pre-
trained language models. Approaches range from lexicon-based methods using resources
such as the NRC Emotion Lexicon [3] to fine-tuned transformer models trained on
affective corpora [4]. More recent work has explored multi-label emotion classification [5],
recognizing that natural language frequently expresses mixed or co-occurring emotions.
ACM integrates both paradigms: a keyword-based scoring component draws on lexical
resources for efficiency and interpretability, while a semantic embedding component using
theall-MiniLM-L6-v2 sentence transformer [6] captures contextual and compositional
affective meaning that surface-level lexica miss.
2.3 Emotional Coherence and AI Safety
The relationship between emotional coherence and AI safety has received comparatively
limited attention. Existing work on AI alignment has focused predominantly on value
alignment, factual accuracy, and the prevention of harmful outputs [7]. However, affective
misalignment—wherein a model’s emotional register is inconsistent with the context—
represents a distinct failure mode. Emotionally incongruent responses can mislead users
about a model’s understanding, erode trust, and in sensitive contexts such as mental
health support, cause harm.
The ACM system directly addresses this gap by providing a principled, mathematically
grounded mechanism for detecting and correcting affective incoherence at the generation
level.

4. System Architecture
ACM is designed as a modular, non-invasive layer that wraps around a transformer-based
language model. The integration follows a sequential pipeline architecture, summarized
below.
Step 1 — Input Processing.The model’s candidate response is tokenized. Keyword
scores skw,eare computed via lexical matching against importance-weighted emotion
dictionaries (keyword importance multipliers range from 0 .85 for curiosity to 0 .95 for joy).
Semantic scoress sem,eare computed via Eq. (2).
Step 2 — Emotion Fusion.Scores are fused via Eq. (1)and cross-modulated via
Eqs. (6)–(7).
Step 3 — Dimensional Computation.Valence Vand arousal Aare computed via

Step 4 — Mismatch Detection.The context mismatch detector computes V C,AD,

Step 5 — Homeostatic Decay.Stored emotional state is updated via Eq. (17), then

Step 6 — Habituation.Frequency-based attenuation is applied via Eq. (21) within a

Step 7 — Alert Classification.Alert level and affective state type are determined via

5. Discussion
5.1 Implications for AI Safety
The ACM framework addresses a class of AI failure modes that existing safety mechanisms
largely overlook:affective misalignment. While current alignment research has made
significant strides in reducing harmful outputs and improving factual accuracy, the
emotional register of AI responses has received comparatively little formal treatment.
Emotionally incoherent AI responses can be harmful in subtle but meaningful ways. In
mental health support contexts, a model that responds to expressions of distress with
affectively neutral or inappropriately positive language may fail to validate the user’s emo-
tional state, potentially worsening outcomes. In educational or therapeutic applications,
9
sustained affective incoherence may erode user trust and reduce the effectiveness of the
interaction.
ACM’s temperature modulation mechanism provides a principled, mathematically grounded
intervention: by reducing generative stochasticity under conditions of detected emotional
incoherence, the system constrains the model toward more conservative, contextually
appropriate outputs. This represents a novel contribution to the AI safety toolkit—a form
ofaffective guardrailingthat operates at the generation level.
5.2 Biological Grounding
A distinctive feature of ACM is its deliberate grounding in biological models of emotion.
The exponential decay model (Eq. (17)) mirrors established psychophysiological models of
emotional recovery [9]. The habituation mechanism (Eq. (21)) instantiates a core principle
of neural adaptation [10]. The valence-arousal dimensional representation draws directly
on Russell’s circumplex model [2].
This biological grounding is not merely cosmetic. It provides principled constraints on
parameter choices and offers a theoretical framework for interpreting the system’s behavior
in terms of established affective science.
5.3 Limitations
Several limitations warrant acknowledgment. First, the current implementation relies on a
fixed set of eight emotion categories; affective states in natural language are considerably
more nuanced and culturally variable. Second, the keyword lexicon and modulation
thresholds are heuristically tuned and may not generalize uniformly across domains or
languages; comprehensive empirical validation on annotated conversational datasets is
required to establish their validity. Third, the semantic embedding component, while
powerful, inherits any biases present in the pre-training data of all-MiniLM-L6-v2 [6].
Fourth, the half-life parameter ( t1/2= 4.0 s) and the emotion-specific decay factors
in Table 1 are initial estimates chosen to operate at conversational-turn timescales;
they do not directly correspond to the longer-horizon emotional dynamics studied in
psychophysiology and require empirical calibration. Finally, the temperature modulation
mechanism assumes a direct relationship between generation temperature and affective
coherence that merits rigorous empirical validation—reducing temperature constrains the
output distribution but does not guarantee improved emotional appropriateness.
6. Conclusion and Future Work
This paper has presented the Affective Coherence Monitoring (ACM), a biologically
inspired mathematical framework for the real-time monitoring, detection, and regulation
of emotional coherence in transformer-based AI systems. ACM integrates hybrid emotion
scoring, dimensional valence-arousal computation, cross-emotion modulation, context
mismatch detection, homeostatic decay, habituation dynamics, alert classification, and
closed-loop temperature modulation into a unified, principled pipeline.
The framework bridges computational affect theory with practical LLM safety mechanisms,
contributing a novel dimension to the AI alignment toolkit.
10
Future work will pursue several directions. A comprehensive quantitative evaluation
across diverse conversational benchmarks will establish formal performance metrics and
provide the empirical grounding needed to validate the heuristic parameters introduced
in this paper. Extension of the emotion taxonomy beyond the current eight categories
will improve expressive coverage. Multilingual adaptation of the keyword lexicon and
centroid embeddings will broaden applicability. Finally, integration studies with different
transformer architectures will assess the generality of the ACM approach.
The ACM represents a step toward AI systems that are not merely factually accurate and
safe, butaffectively coherent—capable of engaging with human emotional experience in a
manner that is consistent, appropriate, and trustworthy.
